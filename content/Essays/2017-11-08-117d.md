Suppose you are a math PhD student at MIT.
Officially, this "costs" \$50K a year in
[tuition](http://web.mit.edu/registrar/reg/costs/graduate/grad_fallspring.html).
Fortunately this number is meaningless, because math PhD students
[serve time as teaching assistants](http://math.mit.edu/academics/grad/financial/index.php)
in exchange for having the nominal sticker price waived.
MIT then provides a stipend of about \$25K a year for these PhD student's living expenses.
This stipend is taxable, but it's small and you'd pay only \$1K-\$2K in federal taxes (about 6%).

The new GOP tax proposal strikes
[26 U.S. Code 117(d)](https://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/26/117)
which would cause the \$50K tuition waiver to _also_ become taxable income:
the PhD student would pay taxes on an "income" of \$75K, at tax brackets of 12% and 25%.
If I haven't messed up the calculation,
for our single PhD student this means
**paying \$10K in federal taxes out of the same \$25K stipend (about 40%)**.

I think a 40% tax rate for a PhD student is a _bit_ unreasonable;
the remaining \$15K a year is not too far from the poverty line.

(The relevant sentence is page 96,
line 20 of the [GOP tax bill](https://scribd.com/document/363309136/Republican-Tax-Bill#fullscreen&from_embed).)
